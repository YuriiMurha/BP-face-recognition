# Literature Review

This chapter reviews the work the thesis builds on: the evolution of face recognition and face detection, the embedding-based FaceNet paradigm that every recognition experiment uses, the transfer-learning techniques used to adapt it, and the way detection and recognition are evaluated. The algorithmic and mathematical detail of the methods actually implemented is given in Chapter 3.

## Face recognition: a brief history

Automatic face recognition has been studied for more than three decades, and the methods that defined each era reflect what was computationally feasible at the time. The modern thread begins with Eigenfaces [CITE: turk1991eigenfaces], which describe a face by its coefficients on the principal components of a set of aligned faces and recognise by nearest-neighbour search. The representation is holistic and collapses when faces are not tightly cropped, frontal, and evenly lit, because PCA preserves illumination variation as eagerly as identity. Fisherfaces [CITE: belhumeur1997fisherfaces] replaced PCA with Linear Discriminant Analysis to isolate identity-relevant directions, improving robustness to illumination but keeping the holistic assumption and a fixed-gallery, closed-set design. Local Binary Patterns [CITE: ahonen2006lbp] introduced the first local representation that scaled, encoding per-pixel intensity contrast into histograms that are fast and approximately illumination-invariant; LBP set the baseline that the first deep methods had to beat.

DeepFace [CITE: taigman2014deepface] beat that baseline by a wide margin, training a nine-layer network on four million images to reach 97.35% verification on Labeled Faces in the Wild, near the human level. FaceNet [CITE: schroff2015facenet] then reframed the problem, replacing classification heads with L2-normalized embeddings trained by triplet loss. From 2016 the field converged on ResNet-style backbones and shifted attention to the loss function and data curation, with angular-margin losses such as ArcFace [CITE: deng2019arcface] pushing LFW verification above 99.8% by 2020. With benchmark headroom largely exhausted, attention moved to fairness, robustness under pose and age, and deployment under constrained compute --- the concerns that motivate this work.

## Face detection: classical to deep

Detection has a more constrained problem --- locate every face and return boxes --- and speed has always mattered because it sits in front of every later stage. Viola and Jones [CITE: viola2001robust] gave the first detector both fast and accurate enough for real use, combining integral images, AdaBoost feature selection, and a rejection cascade; OpenCV's Haar cascade descends from it but degrades under rotation, occlusion, and textured backgrounds. The HOG descriptor with a linear SVM [CITE: dalal2005hog] gave a second classical pillar, and dlib's face detector [CITE: king2009dlib] is its canonical application to faces --- more robust than Haar but slower per frame.

The shift to deep detectors came with MTCNN [CITE: zhang2016mtcnn], a three-stage cascade that jointly predicts boxes and five landmarks; its image pyramid recovers small and occluded faces, giving high recall at the cost of hundreds of milliseconds per frame on CPU. Single-shot detectors then closed the speed gap: RetinaFace [CITE: deng2020retinaface] reaches state-of-the-art accuracy in one pass, and BlazeFace [CITE: bazarevsky2019blazeface] pushes speed to its limit with depthwise-separable convolutions [CITE: howard2017mobilenets] and an anchor-free head, clearing 200 FPS even on CPU but missing distant or oblique faces because it has no pyramid. The detectors evaluated here --- Haar and HOG (classical), MTCNN (deep cascade), and BlazeFace (single-shot mobile) --- were chosen to span this speed--quality curve; each occupies a different operating point, quantified in Chapter 6.

## FaceNet and the embedding paradigm

FaceNet [CITE: schroff2015facenet] is the keystone of this thesis: every recognition experiment uses an InceptionResNetV1 backbone derived from it. Its argument is that a recognition system should not learn class-specific decision boundaries but a single mapping from images to an embedding space where squared Euclidean distance is small for the same person and large for different people. Recognition is then nearest-neighbour search, verification a thresholded distance, and enrolling a new identity a database insertion rather than a model update --- the property that makes the paradigm right for surveillance galleries that grow at runtime. The embedding is L2-normalized onto the unit hypersphere, which separates direction (identity) from magnitude (image-quality artefacts). FaceNet reached 99.63% on LFW; the InceptionResNetV1 weights used here output 512 dimensions and were trained with triplet loss and semi-hard mining, whose importance was later underlined by Hermans and colleagues [CITE: hermans2017triplet].

## Transfer learning and fine-tuning

Adapting a pre-trained backbone to a small domain without destroying it is the methodological core of the experiments. Early CNN layers learn near-task-agnostic features and late layers task-specific ones, with transferability decaying smoothly with depth [CITE: yosinski2014transferable]; off-the-shelf pre-trained features are often hard to beat even with no fine-tuning [CITE: razavian2014cnn], which is why feature extraction with a frozen backbone is the natural starting point. Unfreezing the backbone risks catastrophic forgetting [CITE: mccloskey1989catastrophic; goodfellow2013catastrophic], in which full-rate fine-tuning overwrites the general-purpose features before the new task is learned. The standard mitigations --- smaller learning rates for lower layers, and unfreezing layers gradually rather than all at once --- come from discriminative fine-tuning and progressive unfreezing, introduced for language models in ULMFiT [CITE: howard2018ulmfit] and used earlier by Felbo and colleagues [CITE: felbo2017deepmoji]. Progressive unfreezing trains the head first, then unfreezes the backbone top-down so deeper, task-specific layers adapt before shallower ones are touched. It is rarely applied to vision because public benchmarks have enough data for simpler full fine-tuning, but it suits small target domains such as surveillance face recognition; Chapter 3 describes the four-phase schedule used here and Chapter 6 reports its result.

## Recognition paradigms and evaluation

The thesis implements both closed-set and open-set recognition over a shared detector. Closed-set recognition assumes every probe is one of the N enrolled identities and is implemented as softmax classification, measured by accuracy and per-class F1; adding an identity means retraining the head. Open-set recognition allows a probe to belong to none of the enrolled identities and is implemented as embedding plus thresholded nearest-neighbour retrieval, measured by True Acceptance Rate at fixed False Acceptance Rate, Equal Error Rate, and ROC area. The distinction matters when reading the literature, because most benchmarks --- LFW, for example --- report 1:1 verification rather than closed-set classification.

Detection is evaluated geometrically. A prediction counts as a true positive when its Intersection over Union with a ground-truth box exceeds a threshold, here 0.5, and precision, recall, and F1 follow from the per-frame counts. This thesis reports precision, recall, F1, and mean IoU on 19 manually annotated surveillance frames; because the set is small, Chapter 6 gives the per-method breakdowns in full rather than a single summary number. For recognition, classification accuracy alone can hide failure modes, so the results also report embedding-geometry measures --- intra- and inter-class distance and their separation ratio --- that describe the structure of the feature space, not just the decision boundary.

## Datasets and gap analysis

Public face datasets are almost all assembled by web scraping, which skews them toward celebrities and the demographics over-represented in English-language press; Buolamwini and Gebru [CITE: buolamwini2018gendershades] showed gender classifiers trained on such data failing far more often on dark-skinned women than on light-skinned men. LFW [CITE: huang2007lfw] remains the standard verification benchmark but saturated above 99% by 2017, and large training corpora such as VGGFace2 [CITE: cao2018vggface2] and the withdrawn MS-Celeb-1M [CITE: guo2016msceleb] trained the weights that most academic work, including this thesis, builds on. For a surveillance system with a small, known population, custom data is necessary precisely because public data does not represent the deployment domain.

This is the gap the thesis addresses. Comparative evaluation of fine-tuning strategies on a small custom dataset is sparse, because most published results use hundreds of thousands to millions of images. The thesis contributes a side-by-side comparison of three FaceNet fine-tuning strategies on a 14-identity surveillance dataset of about 7,000 images, evaluated by both closed-set accuracy and embedding geometry, together with a ground-truth detection benchmark on annotated indoor frames. The finding that progressive unfreezing wins on accuracy while triplet loss wins on embedding separation shows that the right strategy depends on the downstream task --- a distinction the larger benchmark literature tends to collapse into a single verification number.
