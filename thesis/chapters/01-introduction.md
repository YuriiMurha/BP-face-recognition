# Introduction {.unnumbered}

Video surveillance is now a routine part of public and private spaces, and the cameras that record it are increasingly expected to interpret what they see rather than simply store footage. Face recognition is one of the central tasks built on that infrastructure: a fixed camera, a face that crosses its field of view, and an identity to confirm or look up. The applications run from access control at a door, through attendance and visitor tracking, to forensic search through recorded video.

The difficulty is that the conditions under which face-recognition models are usually evaluated are not the conditions in which a surveillance system has to work. Benchmark images tend to be curated, well lit, and frontal; surveillance footage is none of those things. Faces appear at oblique angles, at a distance, under uneven artificial light, and on cameras chosen for coverage rather than image quality. A model that scores above 99% on a public benchmark can lose a large fraction of that accuracy when it is pointed at a real camera, and the gap is not closed by reusing a pre-trained model unchanged.

This thesis takes the practical view of a small project: how well can a deployable face-recognition system be built from openly available components, modest hardware, and a small custom dataset, and what trade-offs does that involve? It treats both halves of the pipeline --- detecting faces and recognizing them --- and measures each against ground truth rather than against itself, so that the reported numbers mean what they say. Privacy is a constraint on the design throughout: the system runs entirely on local hardware and sends no images to third-party services.

The rest of the thesis is organised as follows. Chapter 1 restates the assignment and how it was met. Chapter 2 reviews the detection and recognition methods the work builds on. Chapter 3 gives the algorithmic and mathematical background for the detectors and the three fine-tuning strategies. Chapter 4 documents the custom dataset and its preprocessing. Chapter 5 describes the implementation and system architecture. Chapter 6 presents the experimental results. The Conclusion summarises the outcomes, limitations, and directions for future work. Appendix A documents the contents of the attached CD, Appendix B is a user manual, and Appendix C a system manual.
