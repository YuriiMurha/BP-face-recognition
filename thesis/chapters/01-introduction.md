# Chapter 1: Introduction

## 1.1 Background

Video surveillance has become an everyday part of public and private spaces. By 2021 there were more than one billion surveillance cameras deployed worldwide, and the global video surveillance market was valued at roughly USD 54 billion in 2023 [CITE: een2023statistics]. The growth of the installed base has been matched by a shift in what cameras are expected to do: rather than simply recording footage for human review, modern systems are expected to interpret the scene in real time, flagging events of interest as they happen [CITE: axis2023aistate].

Face recognition is one of the central interpretive tasks built on top of this infrastructure. The pairing is natural --- a fixed camera and a face that crosses its field of view --- and the applications range from access control at a controlled doorway, through attendance and visitor tracking, to forensic search through recorded footage. Industry surveys report that the share of video surveillance buyers who have acquired AI-related capabilities rose by roughly seven percentage points between 2021 and 2023, with most of that growth driven by analytics that operate on detected people and faces [CITE: axis2023aistate]. Deep learning has driven this turn from research curiosity to deployed product: convolutional networks trained on tens of millions of face images now achieve verification accuracies above 99% on standard benchmarks such as Labeled Faces in the Wild [CITE: wang2021deepface, schroff2015facenet].

What is not always visible in benchmark numbers is the distance between the conditions under which those numbers were obtained and the conditions in which a deployed system has to work. Benchmark images are largely curated, well-lit, frontal, and drawn from web sources. Surveillance footage is none of those things. Faces appear at oblique angles, at distances of several meters, under uneven artificial lighting, and on cameras whose colour, focus, and frame rate were not chosen with face recognition in mind. The performance of state-of-the-art models drops sharply when they are moved across this domain gap, and the gap is not closed by simply pointing a pre-trained model at a new camera [CITE: wang2021deepface, deng2017crossage].

## 1.2 Motivation

This thesis is motivated by the practical question of how to build a deployable face recognition system for indoor surveillance using publicly available models, modest hardware, and a small custom dataset. The headline accuracy figures published with large face recognition models depend on training corpora of millions of images. Most real surveillance installations have access to nothing comparable: a typical site has tens of identities of interest and at most a few thousand frames per identity, often captured from a single camera. The interesting research question is not whether a 99% benchmark accuracy is achievable in principle, but whether a system built with the resources actually available to a small project can come close to that accuracy on its own deployment domain, and what trade-offs that involves.

A second motivation is reproducibility. Face recognition is a domain where the published numbers are often hard to reproduce because the underlying models, datasets, and evaluation protocols are partly proprietary or inconsistently documented. The work presented here is deliberately constructed around open components --- MediaPipe, FaceNet, dlib, MTCNN, TensorFlow --- combined through a configuration-driven plugin system, with the goal that every reported number can be regenerated from the repository. A third motivation, and a real constraint on the design, is privacy. Under the General Data Protection Regulation, a facial template processed for the purpose of identification is special-category personal data [CITE: gdpr2016article9]. Any system that handles such data has to be deployable on local hardware without sending images to third-party APIs, and the architecture chosen here reflects that requirement.

## 1.3 Problem Statement and Research Questions

The problem addressed by this thesis is the design, implementation, and evaluation of a face recognition pipeline suitable for real-time indoor surveillance, built from openly available components and trained on a small custom dataset. The pipeline must support both closed-set identification against a fixed set of enrolled people and open-set verification against a database that can grow at runtime. It must run at video rate on a CPU and remain interpretable enough that its accuracy can be measured against ground-truth annotations rather than against itself. The work pursues four research questions:

- **RQ1.** Which face detection method achieves the best trade-off between speed and ground-truth detection quality on indoor surveillance frames, when quality is measured by precision, recall, and F1 against manually annotated bounding boxes at an intersection-over-union threshold of 0.5?
- **RQ2.** Can transfer learning of a pre-trained FaceNet model achieve more than 95% closed-set classification accuracy on a custom 14-class surveillance dataset of approximately 7,000 images, and which fine-tuning strategy delivers that accuracy most reliably?
- **RQ3.** Does fine-tuning the FaceNet backbone with triplet loss produce a more strongly separated embedding space than fine-tuning it with cross-entropy classification, and does the better embedding geometry translate into better open-set verification performance measured by equal error rate and true acceptance rate at fixed false acceptance rate?
- **RQ4.** How robust are the reported accuracy figures to random seed variation across train/validation/test splits, and what is the realistic uncertainty around the headline numbers?

The first question concerns the front end of the pipeline; the second and third concern the recognition stage; the fourth is a methodological question that anticipates the cross-validation analysis used to qualify the headline results.

## 1.4 Thesis Contributions

The thesis makes the following contributions:

1. **A dual-paradigm face recognition system** that shares a single detection front end between closed-set classification and open-set embedding retrieval. The two paradigms are implemented as separate entry points (`main.py` and `closed_set_main.py`) over a common service layer, so that the same detector, preprocessing pipeline, and registration tools are reused across both modes.
2. **A ground-truth evaluation of four face detectors** --- MediaPipe BlazeFace, MTCNN, Haar Cascade, and dlib HOG --- on 19 manually annotated indoor surveillance frames containing 26 face boxes. The evaluation uses greedy IoU matching at 0.5 and reports precision, recall, F1, mean IoU, and wall-clock latency. The headline finding is that MTCNN dominates on quality (F1 = 0.706) while MediaPipe dominates on speed (238 FPS) but pays a steep recall penalty, and that Haar and dlib HOG produce mostly false positives on this footage.
3. **A side-by-side comparison of three FaceNet fine-tuning strategies** on a custom 14-class dataset of 7,080 images: feature extraction with a frozen backbone (92.84%), progressive unfreezing in four phases with decaying learning rates (99.15%), and metric learning with random-mined triplet loss (94.63%). Progressive unfreezing is identified as the best closed-set strategy on this data and is selected as the production default.
4. **An embedding-geometry analysis** of the 512-dimensional FaceNet backbone after each fine-tuning strategy. Triplet loss produces the most strongly separated embedding space (separation ratio 3.72 versus 1.90 for progressive unfreezing), even though it loses on classification accuracy. The result clarifies that the right metric for choosing a fine-tuning strategy depends on the downstream task.
5. **An open-set verification evaluation** on 5,000 positive and 5,000 negative pairs sampled from the test set, reporting equal error rate, true acceptance rate at fixed false acceptance rates, and the area under the receiver-operating-characteristic curve. Progressive unfreezing achieves the lowest EER (0.090) on in-distribution pairs.
6. **A plugin-based architecture** with a YAML model registry that supports more than 20 detector/recognizer combinations and is portable across Windows (CPU) and WSL2 (GPU) environments. Adding a new model is a configuration change rather than a code change, and the registry is what allowed the comparative studies above to be carried out reproducibly.

## 1.5 Thesis Structure

The remainder of the thesis is organized into seven chapters. Chapter 2 reviews the literature on face detection, deep face recognition, transfer learning, and metric learning that the system draws on. Chapter 3 describes the software tools and libraries used in the implementation. Chapter 4 presents the mathematical and algorithmic foundations of the detection methods and the three fine-tuning strategies that are compared. Chapter 5 documents the custom dataset, including its construction, class distribution, and preprocessing pipeline, alongside the public LFW dataset used for auxiliary experiments. Chapter 6 covers the software implementation, including the plugin system, service layer, and dual-paradigm entry points. Chapter 7 presents the experimental results, structured around the four research questions: detector benchmarking against ground truth, the three-way comparison of fine-tuning strategies, per-class accuracy and class-imbalance analysis, and the embedding-geometry and verification studies. Chapter 8 concludes with a synthesis of the findings, practical deployment recommendations, an honest account of the limitations of the work, and directions for future research.
