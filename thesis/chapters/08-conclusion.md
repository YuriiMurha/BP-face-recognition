# Conclusion {.unnumbered}

This thesis set out to build and evaluate a face detection and recognition system for real-time indoor surveillance, using open components and a small custom dataset. All four assignment goals were met: the relevant detection and recognition methods were surveyed and implemented, a 14-identity dataset was curated from real cameras and augmented to about 7,000 images, multiple models were trained and compared under a deployment-oriented evaluation, and the system was documented in a user and a system manual.

The main results are concrete. Among detectors, MediaPipe BlazeFace is the only option that clears real-time rates comfortably (238 FPS) but pays for it in recall, while MTCNN gives the best detection quality (F1 = 0.71) at a few frames per second; Haar Cascade and dlib HOG are dominated on both axes. Among recognition strategies, progressive unfreezing of the FaceNet backbone reached 99.2% closed-set accuracy on the single split and was selected as the production default, ahead of frozen transfer learning (92.8%) and triplet-loss fine-tuning (94.6%). Triplet loss produced the most separated embedding space even though it lost on accuracy, a reminder that the right training objective depends on the downstream task.

The work has clear limits. All recognition results come from one 14-identity dataset, so they describe performance on that deployment rather than general accuracy. The open-set verification was measured on pairs drawn from the same identities used in training, which flatters the closed-set-trained model; a held-out benchmark with disjoint identities would be a fairer test. Five-seed cross-validation put the progressive-unfreezing accuracy nearer 94% on average, so the 99.2% single-split figure should be read as an optimistic upper bound rather than expected performance.

Future work follows from those limits: evaluation across different cameras and unseen identities, semi-hard triplet mining, scaling to more identities, and edge-deployment optimisation such as quantization and pruning. As built, the system shows that careful adaptation of a pre-trained model, rather than training from scratch, is enough to reach useful accuracy on a small surveillance dataset with commodity hardware.
