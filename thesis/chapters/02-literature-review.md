# Chapter 2: Literature Review

This chapter places the thesis in the context of three intersecting bodies of work: face detection, face recognition, and the transfer-learning techniques used to adapt large pre-trained models to small target domains. The treatment is historical and analytical rather than mathematical; the equations that define the methods used in the implementation are derived in Chapter 4. Here the goal is to explain how each method came to exist, what problem it was designed to solve, and where its successors made it obsolete or relegated it to a fallback role.

The chapter is organized around the natural decomposition of the pipeline. Section 2.1 traces the evolution of face recognition algorithms from holistic statistical methods in the early 1990s to modern angular-margin losses. Section 2.2 does the same for detection, from Viola--Jones to lightweight mobile detectors. Section 2.3 focuses on FaceNet [CITE: schroff2015facenet] because it is the backbone used throughout the experiments. Section 2.4 examines loss functions in detail, since the choice of loss is what distinguishes one face recognition system from another at the architectural level. Section 2.5 covers transfer learning and fine-tuning strategies, the methodological core of the thesis's experimental contribution. Sections 2.6 through 2.9 cover paradigms (open-set versus closed-set), evaluation metrics, public datasets, and the privacy and regulatory framework that constrains any face-recognition system deployed in Europe. Section 2.10 closes with a short gap analysis.

## 2.1 Face Recognition: a brief historical perspective

Automatic face recognition has been an active research problem for more than three decades. The methods that defined each era reflect what was computationally feasible and what theoretical apparatus was available at the time.

The modern thread begins with Eigenfaces [CITE: turk1991eigenfaces]. Turk and Pentland showed that a small set of principal components, computed by applying Principal Component Analysis to a set of aligned face images, captures enough variance to support recognition by nearest-neighbour search in the projected space. The method is holistic: a face is described by a single vector of coefficients on the global principal components rather than by local features such as eye corners or nose shape. Eigenfaces work well when faces are tightly cropped, frontal, and illuminated consistently, and they collapse when any of those conditions fail. The PCA basis maximizes total variance, which has the side effect of preserving variation due to illumination just as eagerly as variation due to identity.

Fisherfaces [CITE: belhumeur1997fisherfaces] addressed exactly that weakness. Belhumeur, Hespanha, and Kriegman replaced PCA with Linear Discriminant Analysis, which finds projection directions that maximize between-class variance relative to within-class variance. The within-class scatter matrix absorbs much of the illumination variation as nuisance, so the projection isolates identity-relevant directions. On the Yale and Harvard databases, Fisherfaces consistently outperformed Eigenfaces under varying illumination and expression. They retain the holistic assumption, however, and they need labelled training data per identity, which limits their use to closed-set scenarios with a fixed gallery.

Local Binary Patterns [CITE: ahonen2006lbp] introduced the first genuinely local representation that scaled. Ahonen, Hadid, and Pietikäinen divided the face into a fixed grid of regions, computed an LBP histogram in each region, and concatenated them into a long feature vector. LBP encodes the local intensity contrast around each pixel as a short binary code, which makes the descriptor approximately invariant to monotonic illumination changes. Recognition reduces to a chi-square or histogram-intersection comparison between LBP vectors. The method is fast, training-free if a chosen distance is used, and reasonably robust to mild pose variation. It set the practical baseline that the first wave of deep learning had to beat.

The first deep learning result that did beat that baseline by a large margin was DeepFace [CITE: taigman2014deepface]. Taigman and colleagues at Facebook trained a nine-layer convolutional network on four million face images of four thousand identities and reached 97.35% verification accuracy on Labeled Faces in the Wild, against a human baseline of 97.53%. Two design choices stand out in retrospect: explicit 3D alignment before the network sees the image, and locally connected layers rather than weight-shared convolutions in the top portion of the network. Both choices were later abandoned by the field --- alignment moved to learned 2D affine warping or was dropped entirely, and locally connected layers proved to be a parameter-budget waste --- but the result demonstrated that deep networks, given enough data, could close the gap to human verification performance.

FaceNet [CITE: schroff2015facenet] then reframed the problem entirely, shifting from softmax classification heads to L2-normalized embeddings trained with triplet loss. VGGFace [CITE: parkhi2015vggface] and its successor VGGFace2 [CITE: cao2018vggface2] published large public datasets and strong baseline networks that became standard for academic comparison. From 2016 onward, the major question stopped being which architecture to use --- the field largely converged on ResNet-style backbones --- and started being which loss function and which data curation strategy to use. Center loss [CITE: wen2016center], SphereFace [CITE: liu2017sphereface], CosFace [CITE: wang2018cosface], and ArcFace [CITE: deng2019arcface] each pushed the loss function in the direction of explicitly enforcing inter-class angular separation. By 2020, ArcFace-trained networks on glints and VGGFace2 routinely cleared 99.8% LFW verification accuracy. The remaining headroom on benchmarks is small enough that the field has shifted attention to fairness, robustness under pose and age variation, and deployment under constrained compute --- the same concerns that motivate this thesis.

## 2.2 Face Detection: classical to deep

Face detection has a slightly different history because the problem statement is more constrained: locate every face in the frame and return bounding boxes. Speed has always mattered for detection because it sits in front of every downstream stage.

Viola and Jones [CITE: viola2001robust] published the first detector that was both fast and accurate enough for real applications. Their contribution combines three ideas. The integral image lets a rectangular sum be computed with four table lookups, so Haar-like features can be evaluated in constant time regardless of window size. AdaBoost selects a small subset of discriminative features from a vast pool. A cascade arrangement of progressively more selective classifiers lets early stages reject obvious non-faces with very little computation, reserving the expensive features for windows that survive. The combined system ran at real-time rates on consumer CPUs of the early 2000s, which is why OpenCV's Haar cascade was the default face detector for more than a decade. Its weakness is well known: the detector is trained on upright frontal faces and degrades quickly under rotation, occlusion, or non-standard imaging conditions. False positives on textured backgrounds, particularly anything containing horizontal stripes near eye height, are a chronic problem.

Histograms of Oriented Gradients [CITE: dalal2005hog] gave detection a second classical pillar. Dalal and Triggs designed HOG for pedestrian detection, where the human body's structure is expressed as a particular pattern of edge orientations across a fixed cell grid. Coupled with a linear SVM, the HOG descriptor produced a detector that was harder to fool than Haar features on cluttered backgrounds. Dlib's face detector [CITE: king2009dlib] is the canonical application of HOG plus linear SVM to faces, in a multi-scale sliding window. It handles mild in-plane rotation better than the Haar cascade and produces fewer false positives, at the cost of substantially higher per-frame latency.

The decisive shift to deep detectors came with MTCNN [CITE: zhang2016mtcnn]. Zhang and colleagues built a three-stage cascade of small convolutional networks --- a proposal network, a refinement network, and an output network --- each successively more discriminative and operating on higher-resolution inputs. The cascade jointly predicts bounding boxes and five facial landmarks, and the landmarks are used downstream for alignment before recognition. MTCNN's headline benefit is recall: the image pyramid plus the cascade structure recover small and partially occluded faces that single-shot detectors miss. Its drawback is speed: traversing the pyramid three times pushes per-frame latency into the hundreds of milliseconds on CPU, which is incompatible with real-time video.

Single-shot detectors closed most of the speed gap. RetinaFace [CITE: deng2020retinaface] unifies face box prediction, 2D landmark localization, and 3D vertex regression into a single forward pass and achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on the WIDER FACE benchmark. BlazeFace [CITE: bazarevsky2019blazeface] takes the speed argument to its conclusion. Bazarevsky and colleagues at Google designed the detector specifically for mobile GPU inference, with depthwise separable convolutions [CITE: howard2017mobilenets] in the backbone, an anchor-free regression head, and a fixed input resolution that lets the entire network fit in cache. The reported sub-millisecond inference on a Pixel-class mobile GPU is achievable in practice, and even on CPU the detector clears 200 FPS on modest hardware. The cost is reduced multi-scale recall: BlazeFace was trained on selfie-camera framing and does not have an explicit pyramid, so distant or sharply oblique faces in surveillance footage are systematically missed. This trade-off is documented quantitatively in Chapter 7 of this thesis.

The collection of detectors used in the experiments was chosen to span this speed--quality curve. Haar Cascade and HOG represent the classical regime. MTCNN represents the multi-stage deep cascade. BlazeFace represents the single-shot mobile-optimized regime. They are not interchangeable; each occupies a different operating point, and the right choice depends on whether the downstream constraint is latency, recall, or precision.

## 2.3 FaceNet and the embedding-based recognition paradigm

The FaceNet paper [CITE: schroff2015facenet] is the keystone of this thesis because every recognition experiment uses an InceptionResNetV1 backbone derived from it. The paper's contribution is conceptual as much as technical. Schroff, Kalenichenko, and Philbin argued that a face recognition system should not learn class-specific decision boundaries at all. It should learn a single mapping from images to a fixed-dimensional embedding space in which the squared Euclidean distance between two embeddings is small when the two images depict the same person and large when they do not. Recognition then reduces to nearest-neighbour search in the embedding space, verification to a thresholded distance, and clustering to standard unsupervised methods.

The architectural choices that made this work are themselves worth noting. The backbone is a 22-layer Inception network in the original paper and an InceptionResNetV1 variant in the most widely deployed weights. Inception blocks apply parallel convolutions at multiple kernel sizes within the same layer and concatenate their outputs, which lets the network attend to features at several receptive-field scales without committing to a single one. Residual connections, added in the InceptionResNetV1 variant, keep gradients stable in a network whose depth would otherwise make them vanish. The final embedding has 128 dimensions in the original paper and 512 in the InceptionResNetV1 variant used in this thesis, and it is L2-normalized so that every embedding lies on the unit hypersphere. Normalizing to unit length decouples direction, which encodes identity, from magnitude, which tends to capture image-quality artefacts and confidence rather than identity.

Training used triplet loss with a margin of 0.2 and a deliberately constructed mining schedule. Random triplets converge too slowly because most triples already satisfy the margin and produce zero gradient. Hardest-negative mining converges fast but is unstable because the hardest negatives early in training are often label noise. Schroff and colleagues used semi-hard mining: negatives that are farther from the anchor than the positive yet still within the margin. The mining strategy was the difference between a model that converged in days and one that did not converge at all, a point later emphasized by [CITE: hermans2017triplet].

FaceNet's 99.63% accuracy on LFW was the first deep result that approached the dataset's labelling-error ceiling. It also demonstrated that an embedding learned for face verification transfers cleanly to identification, clustering, and open-set recognition, all without retraining a classifier head. That property is what makes embedding-based recognition the right paradigm for surveillance applications where the gallery may grow at runtime: registering a new identity is a database insertion rather than a model update.

## 2.4 Loss functions for face recognition

The choice of loss function dominates the design of a face recognition system once the backbone is settled. Each major loss family addresses a specific weakness of its predecessor, and the progression is best understood as a sequence of geometric constraints applied to the embedding space.

### 2.4.1 Softmax cross-entropy

Softmax cross-entropy is the natural starting point and the baseline that every other loss tries to beat. Given an input $x$, the network produces logits $z_i$ for each class $i$, and the posterior is $P(c_i \mid x) = \exp(z_i) / \sum_j \exp(z_j)$. The loss is the negative log-likelihood of the true class. Softmax cross-entropy is stable, easy to optimize, and produces a smooth gradient that scales gracefully to thousands of classes. Its weakness is that it does not enforce any geometric separation between classes in the feature space. Two classes can sit arbitrarily close together as long as their logits differ enough to push the softmax probability above the decision threshold. The features that result are discriminative but not necessarily compact within a class or well separated between classes --- a structural property that matters more for open-set tasks, where new identities have to fall outside every existing cluster, than for closed-set classification.

### 2.4.2 Triplet loss

Triplet loss [CITE: schroff2015facenet] attacks the geometric problem directly. A triplet $(x^a, x^p, x^n)$ contains an anchor, a positive sample of the same identity, and a negative sample of a different identity. The loss requires the squared distance from anchor to negative to exceed the squared distance from anchor to positive by at least a margin $\alpha$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{triplet}} = \big[\, \|f(x^a) - f(x^p)\|_2^2 - \|f(x^a) - f(x^n)\|_2^2 + \alpha \,\big]_+.$$

The hinge form makes the loss zero whenever the constraint is satisfied, so easy triplets produce no gradient and the optimizer focuses on triples that are currently in violation. Geometrically, the margin forces each identity cluster to be separated from every other cluster by at least $\alpha$ in the embedding space.

Triplet loss has two well-known practical problems. The first is mining. The number of possible triplets grows as $O(N^3)$ in the number of training samples, and the vast majority are uninformative once the model has converged on the easy cases. Random mining wastes capacity; hardest-negative mining is unstable; and semi-hard mining requires a batch large enough to contain meaningful negatives, which couples batch size to optimization quality. Hermans, Beyer, and Leibe [CITE: hermans2017triplet] showed that batch-hard mining (selecting the hardest positive and hardest negative within each batch) is competitive with offline semi-hard mining if the batch is constructed carefully, and they argued that triplet loss had been unfairly dismissed in person re-identification because of mining weaknesses rather than the loss itself. The second problem is slow convergence. Triplet loss models typically need an order of magnitude more epochs than softmax models to reach comparable accuracy, because each gradient step only updates the network on the few violating triplets in the batch.

### 2.4.3 Contrastive loss and N-pair loss

Contrastive loss [CITE: hadsell2006contrastive] is an older relative of triplet loss that operates on pairs. A positive pair is pulled together; a negative pair is pushed apart, but only up to a fixed margin. It is simpler to implement than triplet loss but suffers from the same mining problem in a more extreme form, because each pair carries even less information than a triplet. N-pair loss [CITE: sohn2016npair] generalizes triplet loss by comparing an anchor against multiple negatives simultaneously in a single update. The intuition is that triplet loss extracts only one bit of information per step --- which of two negatives is closer to the anchor --- while N-pair extracts $\log_2 N$ bits by ranking N-1 negatives at once. The practical effect is faster convergence at the same batch size, at the cost of slightly more complex batch construction.

### 2.4.4 Center loss

Center loss [CITE: wen2016center] takes a different angle. Wen and colleagues observed that softmax cross-entropy already does a passable job of inter-class separation but does little for intra-class compactness. They proposed an auxiliary term that maintains a centre vector for each class in the feature space and penalizes the distance from each sample to its class centre:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{center}} = \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \|f(x_i) - c_{y_i}\|_2^2.$$

The centres are updated by exponential moving average from the samples themselves. Combined with softmax as a joint loss with a small weight on the centre term, center loss produces feature distributions that are visibly tighter within each class than softmax alone, and the combination has been the bedrock of several follow-up methods.

### 2.4.5 Angular-margin losses: SphereFace, CosFace, ArcFace

The angular-margin family reformulates softmax in a way that allows an explicit margin to be inserted between classes in angular space. The key observation is that if the classifier weight vectors and the feature vectors are both L2-normalized, the logit $z_i = w_i^\top f(x)$ reduces to the cosine of the angle $\theta_i$ between $w_i$ and $f(x)$, scaled by a constant. Softmax decisions then depend purely on angles, and the natural way to enforce separation is to require a minimum angular gap between the correct class and the next-best class.

SphereFace [CITE: liu2017sphereface] was the first to articulate this geometry clearly. Its A-Softmax loss multiplies the angle of the correct class by an integer $m$, so the decision boundary tilts in favour of the correct class by an angular margin proportional to $m$. The multiplicative margin is mathematically clean but numerically unstable, because $\cos(m\theta)$ is non-monotonic on $[0, \pi]$ and the loss surface contains gradient discontinuities. CosFace [CITE: wang2018cosface] replaced the multiplicative margin with an additive cosine margin: the cosine of the correct-class angle is reduced by a constant $m$ before the softmax, so the network has to push the correct-class cosine to $\cos\theta - m$ rather than $\cos\theta$ to achieve the same softmax output. This change makes the loss well-behaved and easy to implement.

ArcFace [CITE: deng2019arcface] refined the geometry once more by applying the margin in angle space rather than in cosine space:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ArcFace}} = -\log \frac{\exp\!\big(s \cos(\theta_{y_i} + m)\big)}{\exp\!\big(s \cos(\theta_{y_i} + m)\big) + \sum_{j \neq y_i} \exp(s \cos\theta_j)},$$

with scale $s$ and additive angular margin $m$. The additive angular margin has a clean geometric interpretation as a geodesic distance on the hypersphere, and on standard benchmarks it consistently edges out CosFace by a small but reproducible margin. By 2020, ArcFace was the de facto default for academic face recognition.

The arc of this evolution is worth pausing on. Softmax produces compact decision boundaries but no geometric separation. Triplet loss enforces separation but is mining-dependent and slow. Center loss tightens intra-class compactness as a softmax add-on. The angular-margin family unifies the two desiderata by working on the unit hypersphere with explicit angular margins, gaining softmax's stability and triplet loss's geometric guarantees in a single objective. The thesis uses softmax for the classifier-head fine-tuning strategies and triplet loss for the metric-learning variant, and the embedding-quality evidence in Chapter 7 confirms what the theory predicts: triplet loss produces a more strongly separated embedding space, but at a measurable cost in classification accuracy on small target domains where mining is hard.

## 2.5 Transfer learning and fine-tuning strategies

The most important practical question for a small project that wants to use FaceNet is not which loss to use --- the pre-trained weights already encode a strong representation --- but how to adapt that representation to a new domain without destroying it.

### 2.5.1 The pre-training plus fine-tuning paradigm

Yosinski, Clune, Bengio, and Lipson [CITE: yosinski2014transferable] quantified what every practitioner suspected: early layers of a deep CNN learn features that are nearly task-agnostic (Gabor-like filters, colour blobs, simple textures), middle layers learn features that are partially task-specific, and late layers learn features that are highly task-specific. Transferability decays smoothly with depth. Their experiments on ImageNet showed that transferring the first three layers of a network trained on one half of ImageNet and retraining only the rest on the other half produces a model that beats one trained from scratch on the target half, even when the two halves contain disjoint classes. The result formalized the intuition behind pre-training: the early layers are doing work that any vision task needs, and there is no reason to rediscover it for every new dataset.

Razavian, Azizpour, Sullivan, and Carlsson [CITE: razavian2014cnn] made the practical case even more starkly. Their paper showed that off-the-shelf CNN features --- the activations of a network trained on ImageNet, used as a feature vector with a linear SVM on top --- beat the state of the art on a remarkable variety of downstream tasks, from object classification through fine-grained recognition to image retrieval, with no fine-tuning at all. The takeaway was that a sufficiently large pre-trained network gives you a feature extractor that is hard to beat with task-specific design.

These two papers together explain why feature extraction with a frozen backbone is the natural starting point for any project that wants to use a large pre-trained model. Its limitation is also visible from Yosinski's results: if a domain-specific feature is not already present in the pre-trained representation, no amount of head retraining can produce it.

### 2.5.2 Catastrophic forgetting

Unfreezing the backbone runs into a problem that connectionist research identified before deep learning was called deep learning. McCloskey and Cohen [CITE: mccloskey1989catastrophic] showed in 1989 that a feedforward network trained sequentially on two tasks tends to overwrite its memory of the first task while learning the second. They called the effect catastrophic interference, and it was severe enough to be cited for years as evidence that connectionist models could not model human sequential learning. Goodfellow, Mirza, Xiao, Courville, and Bengio [CITE: goodfellow2013catastrophic] revisited the phenomenon for modern deep networks and found that it persists, though dropout mitigates it substantially and the magnitude depends on how similar the two tasks are.

For transfer learning specifically, catastrophic forgetting is the failure mode in which fine-tuning a pre-trained backbone with the same learning rate used for the head erases the general-purpose features the network started with. The new task overwrites the old representation before the network can learn anything useful, and the result is a model that performs worse than feature extraction would have. The mitigations are well known: smaller learning rates for backbone layers than for head layers, freezing the bottom layers entirely, or unfreezing layers gradually rather than all at once.

### 2.5.3 Discriminative fine-tuning and slanted learning rates

Howard and Ruder's ULMFiT paper [CITE: howard2018ulmfit] introduced two techniques in a natural language context that have since been adapted to vision. Discriminative fine-tuning assigns a different learning rate to each layer of the network, with deeper layers getting larger rates and shallower layers smaller ones. The intuition is that shallower layers encode features that are nearly task-agnostic and should be preserved, while deeper layers are task-specific and need to change the most. A typical schedule decreases the learning rate by a constant factor of 2.6 from layer to layer. Slanted triangular learning rates ramp the overall learning rate up linearly for a short warmup, then down for the remainder of training. The combination, on six text classification tasks, beat training-from-scratch baselines with two orders of magnitude less data.

### 2.5.4 Progressive unfreezing

Progressive unfreezing is the most aggressive version of the discriminative fine-tuning idea. Felbo and colleagues [CITE: felbo2017deepmoji] used it in their DeepMoji emoji-sentiment model: the head is trained first while the backbone is frozen, then the top layer of the backbone is unfrozen and the model is trained again, then the next layer, and so on, until the entire network is being updated. Howard and Ruder's ULMFiT paper made the same technique standard for NLP transfer learning. The technique has two effects in combination. By stabilizing the head before any backbone updates flow through it, gradient signals to the backbone are sensible from the first step rather than noisy. By unfreezing top-down, deeper layers get to adapt to the new task before shallower (more task-agnostic) layers are touched, so general-purpose features are preserved.

Progressive unfreezing has not been widely adopted in the vision literature, in part because the public vision benchmarks tend to have enough labelled data that simpler full-network fine-tuning suffices. On small target domains --- which surveillance face recognition is a classic example of --- the technique has shown notable benefit. In this thesis, progressive unfreezing in four phases with learning rates decaying from $10^{-3}$ to $10^{-6}$ produced a 99.15% closed-set classification accuracy on a 14-class custom dataset of approximately 7,000 images, compared to 92.84% for the frozen-backbone baseline. The result is consistent with the broader transfer-learning literature even though the application domain is different.

## 2.6 Open-set versus closed-set face recognition

Most face recognition literature implicitly assumes one of two scenarios. Closed-set recognition assumes every probe at inference time belongs to one of the $N$ identities seen during training: the model returns the most likely class. Open-set recognition lifts that assumption: a probe may belong to one of the enrolled identities or to none of them, and the model must be able to reject unknowns. The two scenarios call for different architectures and different evaluation protocols.

Closed-set recognition is most naturally implemented as classification. The final layer is a softmax over the $N$ enrolled identities, and the loss is categorical cross-entropy. Accuracy on a held-out test set drawn from the same $N$ identities is the natural metric. Adding a new identity requires retraining the softmax layer, or at minimum reinitializing the output weights for the new class and continuing training.

Open-set recognition is most naturally implemented as embedding plus retrieval. The model maps each face to a fixed-dimensional embedding, registered identities are stored as embedding vectors in a database, and recognition reduces to nearest-neighbour search with a threshold for rejection. Adding a new identity is a database insertion. The evaluation protocols are different: open-set systems are typically measured by True Acceptance Rate at fixed False Acceptance Rate, by Equal Error Rate, or by ROC area, all of which measure how cleanly the system separates genuine matches from imposters across a threshold sweep.

This thesis implements both paradigms over a common detection front end. The closed-set pipeline uses a classifier head fine-tuned on the 14-identity custom dataset and is best for fixed, small galleries where retraining is acceptable. The open-set pipeline uses the raw FaceNet embedding plus a CSV-backed database and is best for surveillance scenarios where the gallery may grow at runtime. The fact that most published face-recognition benchmarks measure verification (1:1 same/different decisions) rather than closed-set classification can mislead a reader expecting to find direct accuracy numbers in the literature; the LFW protocol, for instance, evaluates pair verification, not classification of LFW's 5,749 identities. The MegaFace benchmark [CITE: kemelmacher2016megaface] explicitly measures identification accuracy in the presence of up to one million distractors, which is closer to the open-set surveillance scenario, but the distractor population is essentially unbounded and the metric is recall at rank one rather than classification accuracy in the closed-set sense.

## 2.7 Evaluation metrics

A face recognition pipeline has two stages and two corresponding metric families. Detection is evaluated geometrically: how well do the predicted boxes overlap the annotated boxes? Recognition is evaluated probabilistically or by threshold sweeps: how often does the model say the right thing, and how confidently?

For detection, the standard primitive is Intersection over Union (IoU): the area of the intersection of the predicted and ground-truth boxes divided by the area of their union. A detection counts as a true positive if it matches a ground-truth box at IoU above a fixed threshold, typically 0.5. From the per-image TP, FP, and FN counts, precision (TP / (TP + FP)), recall (TP / (TP + FN)), and F1 (the harmonic mean) follow directly. Mean Average Precision averages precision across recall levels and is the dominant metric for detection in datasets such as WIDER FACE. This thesis reports precision, recall, F1, and mean IoU on a manually annotated set of 19 surveillance frames, which is small enough that single-number summaries are sometimes misleading; the per-method breakdowns in Chapter 7 are accordingly given in full.

For recognition, the relevant metrics depend on the paradigm. Closed-set classification reports overall accuracy, per-class precision, recall, and F1, and a macro-averaged F1 that weights every identity equally. Confusion matrices are useful when class imbalance is severe because they reveal which classes are mistaken for which. Verification reports True Acceptance Rate at fixed False Acceptance Rates (typically $10^{-3}$ and $10^{-4}$), Equal Error Rate (the point where TAR = FAR), and ROC area. DET curves are sometimes preferred over ROC curves in biometrics because they spread out the regime of low false acceptance rates that matters operationally.

A pure benchmark accuracy number rarely tells the whole story, because two systems can reach the same headline accuracy with very different failure modes. A system that achieves 99% accuracy with the remaining 1% concentrated on a single identity is not equivalent to one whose errors are uniformly distributed. The embedding-geometry metrics used in Chapter 7 (intra-class distance, inter-class distance, separation ratio, silhouette score) complement classification accuracy by characterizing the structure of the feature space rather than the decision boundary, and they make it possible to see that the triplet-loss model in this thesis produces a stronger embedding space even though its classification accuracy is lower.

## 2.8 Datasets in the literature

Public datasets have shaped face recognition more than any single algorithm. The training corpus a network sees determines what it can recognize, and the evaluation set determines how the community ranks methods against each other.

Labeled Faces in the Wild [CITE: huang2007lfw] was the first widely adopted unconstrained benchmark. Released in 2007 by Huang, Ramesh, Berg, and Learned-Miller, it contains 13,233 images of 5,749 identities scraped from the web, with poses, lighting, and backgrounds that vary from one image to the next. LFW set the convention of measuring 1:1 verification on a fixed list of 6,000 image pairs, half matched and half mismatched. Its weakness as a benchmark is that the test set is small relative to modern models' capacity, and accuracy on LFW saturated above 99% by 2017. CASIA-WebFace [CITE: yi2014casia] was the first large public training set, with 494,414 images of 10,575 identities, released in 2014. It was the standard training corpus for academic face recognition for several years and is still used for ablation experiments. MS-Celeb-1M [CITE: guo2016msceleb] pushed scale further, releasing ten million images of one million celebrities in 2016. The dataset was later withdrawn by Microsoft over concerns about consent, but it had already trained the network weights that became the basis for most subsequent academic work, including the InceptionResNetV1 weights used in this thesis. VGGFace [CITE: parkhi2015vggface] and VGGFace2 [CITE: cao2018vggface2] are the Oxford VGG group's contributions, with VGGFace2 in particular emphasizing pose and age variation across its 3.31 million images of 9,131 identities. MegaFace [CITE: kemelmacher2016megaface] is an evaluation benchmark rather than a training set, and it stress-tests identification accuracy at scale by adding up to one million distractor identities to the gallery.

A pattern across these datasets is worth noting. Almost all of them are assembled by scraping the web, which skews them heavily toward celebrities and toward the demographic groups that are over-represented in English-language press photography. Buolamwini and Gebru [CITE: buolamwini2018gendershades] showed that gender classifiers trained on such corpora fail far more often on dark-skinned women than on light-skinned men --- error rates of 34.7% versus 0.8% on the three commercial systems they tested. The bias has nothing to do with the algorithms and everything to do with the training data. For a surveillance application with a small, known target population, custom data is necessary precisely because public data does not represent the deployment domain. This thesis assembles a 48-identity corpus from three sources --- a frontal webcam, a ceiling-mounted IP camera, and a filtered LFW subset --- precisely to cover capture conditions that any single public source would miss.

## 2.9 Privacy, ethics, and regulation

A face recognition system that works is also a system that can be misused. The European regulatory framework treats facial templates with a degree of caution that reflects this.

The General Data Protection Regulation [CITE: gdpr2016article9] designates biometric data processed for the purpose of uniquely identifying a natural person as a special category of personal data under Article 9. Processing such data requires either the data subject's explicit consent or one of a small set of statutory exceptions, of which the most relevant in a research context is substantial public interest. The practical implications for a thesis project are concrete: subjects whose faces are captured must consent to the capture and to the specific purpose of the research; the data must be stored locally rather than uploaded to third-party cloud services; and the system must support the right to erasure if a subject withdraws consent.

The Artificial Intelligence Act [CITE: aiact2024] tightens this framework specifically for AI systems. Real-time remote biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces by law enforcement is prohibited except for a narrow set of high-impact use cases (missing persons, abduction victims, imminent terrorist threats, identification of serious-crime suspects). Biometric identification systems more generally are classified as high-risk under Annex III, which triggers requirements for conformity assessment, risk management, data governance, human oversight, and post-market monitoring. The Act entered into force on 1 August 2024 and becomes fully applicable on 2 August 2026, with the high-risk biometric provisions applying from 2 December 2027. A small academic thesis is well below the threshold at which conformity assessment is required, but the spirit of the regulation --- that biometric identification systems should be auditable, accountable, and constrained to legitimate purposes --- is what guides the choice to keep all processing local in this work.

Beyond the legal framework, the fairness question raised by Buolamwini and Gebru [CITE: buolamwini2018gendershades] applies to any face recognition system that will be used outside the narrow demographic of its training corpus. A surveillance system trained on a 14-identity local dataset has the opposite problem from a commercial system trained on millions of celebrities: it is unlikely to generalize to anyone outside its enrolled identities. The advantage of this for a thesis project is that the system can be made to fail safely by rejecting any face whose embedding distance exceeds a threshold. The disadvantage is that the system is useless for any subject not in its database, which is a feature for some deployments (closed-circuit attendance, controlled-access security) and a limitation for others (forensic search through unstructured footage). The system implemented in this thesis is designed for the first kind of use, and the chapter on results documents both how it performs on its enrolled identities and where its limits lie.

## 2.10 Summary and gap analysis

The literature reviewed in this chapter establishes the components from which the thesis is built. Detection has a well-mapped speed--quality trade-off, and the four detectors evaluated span that trade-off comprehensively. Recognition has converged on embedding-based methods with FaceNet's InceptionResNetV1 backbone and L2-normalized output as a standard reference point. Loss functions have evolved from softmax through triplet loss to angular-margin variants, each addressing a specific geometric weakness of its predecessor. Transfer learning, well studied in natural language processing, offers techniques --- discriminative fine-tuning, progressive unfreezing --- that have not been systematically compared on small-data face recognition in the public literature.

The gap this thesis addresses sits inside the last of these observations. Most published face recognition results are obtained on training corpora of hundreds of thousands to millions of images, and the comparative evaluation of fine-tuning strategies on a small custom dataset is correspondingly sparse. The thesis contributes a side-by-side comparison of three FaceNet fine-tuning strategies on a 14-identity surveillance dataset of approximately 7,000 images, evaluated both by closed-set classification accuracy and by embedding-space geometry. The result that progressive unfreezing dominates classification accuracy while triplet loss dominates embedding-space separation suggests that the right strategy depends on the downstream task --- a distinction that is easy to miss when reading the larger benchmark literature, which collapses both metrics into a single 1:1 verification number. The detection-side contribution is the use of manually annotated ground truth on indoor surveillance frames rather than the dataset-internal evaluation that dominates the literature. Together these pieces give a small-scale but complete picture of what a deployable face recognition system looks like in the regime where the training data is scarce and the deployment domain is fixed.
